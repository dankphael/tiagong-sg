# Romanization Golden Standard for tiagongSG

This document defines the canonical romanization system for each of Singapore's five major Chinese dialects. All dictionary entries, inline phrase cards, and learning materials on tiagongSG must adhere to these standards.

**Authority**: This document is the single source of truth. If any romanization in the codebase conflicts with these standards, this document takes precedence.

---

## 1. Hokkien — Pe̍h-ōe-jī (POJ / 白話字)

**System**: Pe̍h-ōe-jī (POJ) — the "Church Romanization"  
**Origin**: Presbyterian missionaries, 1800s (Bible translation 1873)  
**Global usage**: 150+ years of Bible, dictionaries, literature (Douglas, Barclay, Maryknoll)  
**Tone marks**: Diacritics placed above vowels (á, à, â, ǎ, ā, etc.) or as combining marks  
**Tone count**: 7 tones (+ 1 checked tone = 8 total)

### Tone markings in POJ

| Tone | Name | Mark | Example | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | High level | none (flat) | ka | home |
| 2 | Rising | á (acute) | ká | family |
| 3 | Returning | à (grave) | kà | to press |
| 5 | Low falling | e̍ (dot above) | ke̍ | to open |
| 7 | High checked | eˉ (macron) or ē | kē | guest |
| 8 | Low checked | e̊ (ring) or o͘ | ko͘ | dry |

**Special notes**:
- Tone 4 doesn't exist in most Hokkien dialects (it's merged with tone 3 or 2)
- Tone marks on **final consonant** if there's no vowel: e.g., `tsiok̚` (stone)
- Nasal vowels use combining `ⁿ`: `kauⁿ` (light), `kiⁿ` (shoulder)
- `ts`, `tsh`, `j`, `tsi`, `tshi` for affricate initials
- Common digraphs: `oa`, `oe`, `oo`, `au`, `ai`, `ia`, `io`, `iu`, `ua`

### POJ examples (common phrases in tiagongSG)

| Phrase | POJ | English |
|---|---|---|
| 你好 | lí-hó | Hello |
| 食飽未 | tsia̍h-pa̍-buē | Have you eaten? |
| 多謝 | to-tsiann | Thank you |
| 再見 | tsuà-kinn | Goodbye |
| 肉干 | ba̍h-kua⁷ or ba̍h-kua | Jerky (ba kua) |
| 虾米 | hê-bí | Dried shrimp (hae bee) |
| 食飯 | tsia̍h-pn̄g | Eat rice (jiak png) |
| 烧饼 | sio-tsiann or sio-piànn | Sesame flatbread (zhor bia) |

## 2. Cantonese — Jyutping (粵拼)

**System**: Jyutping (粵拼) — Linguistic Society of Hong Kong  
**Origin**: 1993 (LSHK standard)  
**Global usage**: Dominant in modern digital dictionaries (CC-Canto, Words.hk, CantoDict, Cantonese.org)  
**Tone marks**: Numbers 1–6 appended to each syllable (or word)  
**Tone count**: 6 tones

### Tone numbering in Jyutping

| Tone # | Pitch | Example | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | High level | si1 | yes |
| 2 | High rising | si2 | matter |
| 3 | Mid level | si3 | is |
| 4 | Low falling | si4 | time |
| 5 | Low rising | si5 | try |
| 6 | Low level | si6 | history |

**Special notes**:
- Tone mark always comes at the **end** of the syllable, even if syllable has consonant cluster: `sik6` (eat)
- Initial consonants: `ts`, `tsh`, `j` for affricates
- Nasal finals: `-m`, `-n`, `-ng`
- Common diphthongs: `ai`, `au`, `ei`, `eu`, `oi`, `ou`, `oe`, `ui`, `un`, `ung`
- **Zero initial** syllable (starts with vowel): `aa`, `aai`, `aam`, `aan`, `aang`, `aap`, `aat`

### Jyutping examples

| Phrase | Jyutping | English |
|---|---|---|
| 你好 | nei5 hou2 | Hello |
| 食咗飯未 | sik6 zo2 faan6 mei6 | Have you eaten? |
| 多謝 | do1 ze6 | Thank you |
| 再見 | zoi3 gin3 | Goodbye |

## 3. Teochew — Peng'im (潮州话拼音方案)

**System**: Peng'im (潮州话拼音方案) — Guangdong Education Department  
**Origin**: 1960 (standardized modern romanization)  
**Global usage**: Academic dictionaries, mainland textbooks, language research  
**Tone marks**: Numbers 1–8 appended to syllable  
**Tone count**: 8 tones (characteristic of Teochew)

### Tone numbering in Peng'im

| Tone # | Pitch | Example | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | High level | si1 | silk |
| 2 | High rising | si2 | try |
| 3 | Mid rising | si3 | matter |
| 5 | Low falling | si5 | dead |
| 6 | Low rising | si6 | time |
| 7 | High checked | sik7 | eat |
| 8 | Low checked | sik8 | color |

**Special notes**:
- Tone 4 is not used in Teochew (rare in Southeast Asian Teochew)
- Checked tones (7, 8) end in `-p`, `-t`, `-k`, `-h`
- Initial consonants similar to Hokkien: `ts`, `tsh`, `j` for affricates
- Nasal vowels: trailing `nn` or `m`
- Common finals: `-a`, `-e`, `-i`, `-o`, `-u`, `-ia`, `-io`, `-ua`, `-ue`

### Peng'im examples

| Phrase | Peng'im | English |
|---|---|---|
| 你好 | lu2 ho2 | Hello |
| 食飽未 | ziah8 ba2 boi6 | Have you eaten? |
| 多謝 | do1 sia6 | Thank you |
| 再見 | zai3 gian3 | Goodbye |

## 4. Hakka — Hakka Pinyin (客家话拼音方案)

**System**: Hakka Pinyin (客家话拼音方案) — PRC standardized system  
**Origin**: 1960s (mainland standard), refined for digital use  
**Global usage**: PRC textbooks, digital resources, academic work  
**Tone marks**: Numbers 1–7 appended to syllable (some systems use 6 tones; 7-tone is more inclusive)  
**Tone count**: 7 tones (East Hakka; West/Meizhou variant has 6)

### Tone numbering in Hakka Pinyin

| Tone # | Pitch | Example | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | High level | ka1 | family |
| 2 | High rising | ka2 | (variant reading) |
| 3 | Low rising | ka3 | (variant reading) |
| 4 | Mid falling | ka4 | (variant reading) |
| 5 | Low level | ka5 | (variant reading) |
| 6 | High checked | kat6 | (checked) |
| 7 | Low checked | kat7 | (checked) |

**Special notes**:
- Like Mandarin Pinyin, but with **7 tones** instead of 4
- Initial consonants: `c`, `ch`, `j` for affricates (unlike POJ `ts`, `tsh`, `j`)
- Finals: `-a`, `-e`, `-i`, `-o`, `-u`, `-ia`, `-ie`, `-io`, `-ua`, `-ue`, `-ui`
- Nasal vowels: `-an`, `-en`, `-in`, `-on`, `-un` (also `-ang`, `-eng`, `-ing`, `-ong`, `-ung`)

### Hakka Pinyin examples

| Phrase | Hakka Pinyin | English |
|---|---|---|
| 你好 | ni5 ho3 | Hello |
| 食飽未 | chit4 po3 mi5 | Have you eaten? |
| 多謝 | to1 tsia6 | Thank you |
| 再見 | tsai3 kien3 | Goodbye |

## 5. Hainanese — Hainan Pinyin (海南话拼音方案)

**System**: Hainan Pinyin (海南话拼音方案) — Guangdong Provincial Education Department  
**Origin**: 1960 (standardized modern romanization)  
**Global usage**: PRC textbooks, academic reference; limited diaspora material  
**Tone marks**: Numbers 1–8 appended to syllable  
**Tone count**: 8 tones

### Tone numbering in Hainan Pinyin

| Tone # | Pitch | Example | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | High level | si1 | silk |
| 2 | High rising | si2 | try |
| 3 | Mid level | si3 | lion |
| 5 | Low falling | si5 | private |
| 6 | Low rising | si6 | time |
| 7 | High checked | sik7 | eat |
| 8 | Low checked | sik8 | (variant) |

**Special notes**:
- Tone 4 is not used (merged with tone 3 or 5)
- Very similar to Teochew Peng'im; Hainanese shares many features with Teochew
- Checked tones (7, 8) end in `-p`, `-t`, `-k`
- Initial consonants: `ts`, `tsh`, `j` for affricates (sometimes `c`, `ch`)
- Common nasal: `-n`, `-m`, `-ng`

### Hainan Pinyin examples

| Phrase | Hainan Pinyin | English |
|---|---|---|
| 你好 | ni2 hau3 or ni2 hau5 | Hello |
| 食飽未 | tsiok7 pau2 mi5 | Have you eaten? |
| 多謝 | to1 sia6 | Thank you |
| 再見 | tsai3 kien3 | Goodbye |

## 7. Implementation Rules

### For `dictionary.json` entries

Each word in the dictionary must have:

```json
{
  "headword": {
    "traditional": "[Chinese characters]",
    "simplified": "[Simplified Chinese]",
    "romanized": "[GOLDEN STANDARD romanization for this dialect]",
    "english_pinyin": null
  },
  "dialect": "[hokkien|cantonese|teochew|hakka|hainanese]",
  "pronunciations": [
    {
      "type": "colloquial",
      "romanization_system": "[poj|jyutping|peng'im|hakka_pinyin|hainan_pinyin]",
      "romanization": "[SAME as headword.romanized]",
      "tone_marks": "[tone system explanation, e.g., 'POJ diacritics' or 'tone numbers 1-6']"
    }
  ],
  // ... other fields
}
```

**Rule**: `headword.romanized` and `pronunciations[0].romanization` must be **identical** and in the **golden standard** for that dialect. No informal Singapore-style romanization in these fields.

### For `page.js` inline arrays

Each phrase card entry must have:

```javascript
{
  phrase: "[GOLDEN STANDARD romanization]",  // e.g., "lí-hó" not "lee ho"
  chinese: "[Chinese characters]",
  meaning: "[English translation]",
  romanisation: "[Informal pronunciation guide for English readers]"  // e.g., "lee ho"
}
```

**Rule**: `phrase` uses the golden standard. `romanisation` is the **informal English-reader pronunciation guide** and is optional — only include if it differs meaningfully from `phrase`.

### For new entries (Phase 4 balancing)

When adding Teochew/Hakka/Hainanese equivalents for Hokkien words:

1. Use the **same Chinese character(s)** if the word exists in that dialect
2. Apply the **golden standard romanization** for that dialect
3. Reuse the English definition and semantic tags
4. Mark `verified_by: null` and `source: "ai_generated"` or update per codebase convention
5. If the word doesn't naturally translate to a dialect, **skip it** (don't force equivalents)

## Appendix: Common Errors to Avoid

| Error | Wrong | Right | Reason |
|---|---|---|---|
| **POJ tone 8** | `ko` or `ko8` | `ko͘` or `ko̊` | POJ uses diacritics, not numbers |
| **Jyutping tone mark** | `ne5i hou2` | `nei5 hou2` | Tone number at **end** of syllable |
| **Teochew lacks tone 4** | `si4` | `si3` or `si5` | Teochew merges tone 4 into 3 or 5 |
| **Hakka pinyin affricate** | `tsi` (POJ style) | `ci` | Hakka Pinyin uses `c`, `ch`, not `ts`, `tsh` |
| **Hainanese nasal** | `sia` | `sia6` | Must include tone number |
| **Mixed systems** | `lí-hó` (POJ) + `si3` (Jyutping) | Use only **one** system per dialect | Golden standard is **per-dialect**, not mixed |
